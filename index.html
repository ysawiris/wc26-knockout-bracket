    How It Works

    The pool, in four steps

    1. 1
      The order was set. The 12 teams entered the snake in last-season finish order — see how it fell on the Draft Recap tab.
    2. 2
      Everyone drafted two. The snake gave each team one stronger and one weaker of the 32 Round-of-32 countries — 24 picks in all.
    3. 3
      Advance to score. Every round your country wins is worth points — R32 3, R16 5, QF 8, SF 13, Final 21 — plus 0.1 per goal.
    4. 4
      Most points wins. Ties break on advancement points, then draft slot. Cards and fouls are shown but never count.

    Scoring, round by round

    Every time one of your countries wins a round, you bank that round's points. The values follow the Fibonacci sequence (3, 5, 8, 13, 21) — so surviving deep is worth far more than a pile of early wins.

    Round your team winsThis winRunning total
    Round of 32+33
    Round of 16+58
    Quarterfinal+816
    Semifinal → reaches Final+1329
    Final — Champion 👑+2150

    The running total is your team's score if that's as far as it gets: out in the R32 = 0, a runner-up that loses the final = 29, a champion = 50. No single team can outscore a champion.

    Goals add up too

    Every goal your country scores = +0.1 points. Goals count in every match they play — even one they lose — so a high-scoring team pays off even on a short run.

    Example: your team scores 3 goals in the Round of 32, then gets knocked out. You bank +0.3 from the goals on top of the 0 advancement — and a team that reaches the quarterfinal scoring 5 along the way earns 3 + 5 + 8 + 0.5 = 16.5.

    Yellow cards, red cards and fouls are tracked on the site for color, but they never score.

    Do you need the champion to win?

    Owning the eventual World Cup winner (worth 50) guarantees you first place — no other pair of teams can mathematically catch a champion.

    But remember: 8 of the 32 countries go undrafted. If a free-pool underdog shocks the world and lifts the trophy, the title is wide open — and whoever holds the runner-up (29) plus a deep semifinalist (16) takes the league. So a finalist usually wins it, but the runner-up and deep teams keep everyone alive to the final whistle.

    World Cup 2026 — the knockout

    The biggest World Cup ever: 48 teams, three hosts (United States, Mexico, Canada), 104 matches. After the group stage, 32 teams reach the brand-new Round of 32 — and that's where our pool lives.

    The bracket is single-elimination: R32 → R16 → quarterfinals → semifinals → final, 31 matches in all. Top seeds can only meet in the final (1v32, 2v31, …). The final is July 19 at MetLife Stadium, New York / New Jersey.

    For the pool, only the knockout counts. You own two countries; you score every round they survive plus a small bonus for the goals they score. The further they go, the more you win.

    The two rankings: FIFA & Elo

    Every country on the site carries two numbers — its FIFA seed (the #) and its Elo rank (Elo). Both try to answer the same question, "how good is this team?", but they are built differently and they often disagree. Here is what each one really is.

    ① The FIFA seed — the #

    This is the team's place in the official FIFA Men's World Ranking. We line the 32 Round-of-32 countries up by it and number them 1–32 — that number is the seed.

    Since 2018 FIFA's ranking is itself an Elo-style points system: after each match a team's points move by P + I × (result − expected), where I is how important the game was (a quiet friendly counts for little, a World Cup knockout for a lot). What makes it FIFA's flavour: it counts only official "A" internationals, it ignores the scoreline (a 1–0 and a 5–0 move you the same), and it has no home-field adjustment. That makes it steady, and a little slow to react.

    ② The Elo rating — Elo

    This is each team's World Football Elo from eloratings.net, an independent rating (Spain tops our field near 2180, the weakest sides sit near 1550). We rank all 32 by it to get the Elo number — and this is the rating the Forecast and odds actually run on.

    Its engine is the classic Elo expectation: E = 1 / (1 + 10^((opponent − you) / 400)). A 400-point gap is roughly 10× the odds; equal ratings are a coin flip. After a game the rating shifts by K × G × (result − E) — and unlike FIFA it rewards the margin (the multiplier G grows with goal difference) and adjusts for home advantage (about +100 to the home side). So it digests more matches and moves faster.

    Why they disagree

    Same logistic maths, different diet. A team racking up big, in-form wins — even in friendlies or qualifiers — climbs Elo quickly while its FIFA points lag behind. Norway is the poster child: a FIFA seed of #24 in our field, but Elo rates them Elo #9 — a genuine top-10 side the official ranking is slow to credit.

    Reading the rank badges

    On the draft board and rosters the two numbers sit together, like #24 Elo 9. The colour of the Elo number gives the verdict at a glance:

    BadgeWhat it means
    Elo greenElo rates the team 3+ places stronger than its FIFA seed — underrated, a draft bargain.
    Elo redElo rates them 3+ places weaker — the FIFA seed is flattering them.
    Elo plainThe two rankings roughly agree (within 2 places).

    And what each number controls in the pool:

    NumberWhat it drives
    # FIFA seedThe strong / weak draft pools (seeds 1–16 vs 17–32) and the bracket's seeding reference.
    Elo rankThe Forecast win probabilities and xG projections — and the badge colour above.

    Worked example. Norway (Elo 1950) meets Ivory Coast (Elo 1700) in the Round of 32 — a 250-point edge. Plug it in: 1 / (1 + 10^((1700 − 1950) / 400)) ≈ 0.81, so the model gives Norway about an 81% chance to advance, despite its modest #24 seed. That gap is exactly why a "weak-pool" Norway pick can be a steal.

    Bottom line: the # seed decides where a team sits in the draft; the Elo rank is the model's read on how good they actually are. When the two diverge, trust Elo for forecasting — and watch for green badges on the draft board.
